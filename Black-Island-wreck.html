About the Black Island Wreck

Located on the eastern side of Malajon Island, also known as Black Island for its distinctive black rocks, the wreck lies adjacent to a stranded coastal ship. The 45-meter (148-foot) long vessel of unknown origin rests upright on a sandy slope, with its bow at 32 meters (105 feet) and stern at 20 meters (66 feet) deep. This site offers marvelous wreck diving opportunities, particularly suitable for beginners, photographers, and night divers. It teems with marine life, including large scorpionfish, red lionfish, batfish in small shoals, and abundant snappers, fusiliers, sea basses, trumpetfish, six-banded, and imperial angelfish. The ship's hull is densely adorned with sponges and hydrozoans. While coral coverage is limited on the west side of Malajon Island, occasional strong currents can attract pelagic species such as hammerheads. Generally, conditions are calm with gentle currents, but caution is advised as the South China Sea is unprotected and can experience rough weather and extreme currents. Visibility averages around 20 meters (66 feet). For safety, avoid diving during severe weather conditions.

What Makes It Special

Unlike other wrecks in Coron Bay, the Black Island Wreck is isolated and provides a more serene and undisturbed experience. Its partially intact structure offers fascinating exploration opportunities, with coral formations and marine life thriving in and around the ship. The backdrop of the dramatic Black Island adds to the site's allure, making it a hidden gem for wreck diving enthusiasts.

Highlights of Diving the Black Island Wreck

  • Depth: 18-32 meters
  • Visibility: 15-30 meters
  • Marine Life: Barracudas, groupers, nudibranchs, and soft corals
  • Unique Features: Remote location, peaceful diving experience, and thriving coral reefs
  • Experience Level: Intermediate to advanced divers

Dive Tips for the Black Island Wreck

The remote location of the Black Island Wreck means divers should plan their trips with experienced operators. Bring a dive light to navigate the interior safely and enjoy close-up views of marine life. The area is usually calm, but always check weather conditions before heading out.
